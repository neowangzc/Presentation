# Speaker Script

## Slide 1: Title

Hi everyone. My name is Wang Zhichao. I am a second-year doctoral student in Computational Humanities and Social Sciences. Today I want to share my research progress and plan.

## Slide 2: Overarching Theme

The big question behind all my work is: how are values and norms formed and changed? I study this from four directions. First, networks and status in cultural fields. Second, online labor markets. Third, AI and LLM social simulation. And fourth, building a human-agent social platform. Let me go through each one.

## Slide 3: I. Study 1 — Centrality, Closure & Award Nominations

The first study asks: how does an actor's position in a collaboration network lead to peer recognition, like award nominations? There is a tension in the theory. Centrality gives you access to resources and information. Closure gives you trust and reputation. I want to know which one matters more for consecration. So far, I have built the full collaboration network, assembled a panel of nomination events, and got some preliminary model results. The next step is to look at how era and genre change the picture. I plan to target a cultural sociology journal.

## Slide 4: I. Study 2 — Juror Ties & Award Outcomes

The second study asks: do social ties between actors and jurors affect who gets nominated and who wins? To identify this, I use the fact that jurors rotate. This gives us exogenous variation — in some years, a given juror is on the panel, and in other years, they are not. I also control strictly for the quality of the work and the actor. So far, I have built the joint juror-actor network, and the early results show significant network effects. The next step is to dig deeper into the research question.

## Slide 5: I. Study 3 — Debut Works & Subsequent Careers

This study asks: do the features of an actor's debut work — things like genre, scale, director prestige, and collaborators — predict their later career? The theory here is about path dependence and cumulative advantage. Early success or early connections may have lasting effects. I have built a feature vector for debut works and run some preliminary predictions for 5 to 10 year outcomes. The early findings show that box office performance of the debut matters, and collaborator prestige at debut also has a lasting effect. I will continue to refine the analysis.

## Slide 6: I. Study 4 — ML for Box Office & Ratings

The fourth study in this part uses machine learning — Gradient Boosting and Random Forest — to predict a film's box office and its ratings. I use SHAP to understand which features matter most, and I compare the drivers of commercial success versus critical reception. The preliminary results are done. The challenge now is that the model is predictive, but the findings need a stronger sociological explanation. So the next step is to anchor the results in theory.

## Slide 7: II. Study 1 — Why Do Women Exit Male-Typed Domains?

Moving to Part Two, online labor markets. The first study asks: why are women underrepresented in male-typed work, especially IT, on online freelance platforms? The core finding is an asymmetric effect. Failure speeds up women's exit from male-typed domains, but success does not equally keep them in. The draft is done and will be presented at PAS 2026. The next step is to collect feedback and submit to a journal.

## Slide 8: II. Study 2 — The Reputation Discount in OLMs

The second study finds that women face a reputation discount in online labor markets. Even with equal experience and ratings, their win rate is systematically lower than men's. The draft is complete. Next, I will collect feedback and submit to a journal.

## Slide 9: III. Study 1 — Steering Vectors & Norm Compliance

Now Part Three, AI and LLM simulation. The first study asks: can we use activation steering to change how an LLM follows norms? I test two kinds of steering vectors — normative expectation vectors and empirical expectation vectors — and see how they affect the model's decisions. The good news is that single-scenario steering works. We can change what the LLM decides. But the bad news is that it does not generalize well across different scenarios. The next steps are to separate norm encoding from scenario-specific features, try joint multi-scenario training, and test whether steering vectors transfer across models.

## Slide 10: III. Study 2 — Extracting Steering Vectors from Human Data

The second study goes further. Can we take human survey data from social science experiments and use it to create steering vectors or fine-tune LLMs, so that the models reproduce human decision patterns? So far, the cross-scenario generalization is still weak. The next step is to explore what the latent space looks like and see if there is something useful for theory.

## Slide 11: III. Study 3 — Norm Emergence in an AI-Agent Social Platform

The third study looks at norm emergence. When you run an AI-agent social platform over time, do norms, language, and culture emerge on their own? Data collection and preliminary processing are done. Next, I want to track text embedding shifts over time, detect the critical points where norms form, compare the results against human experiments, and eventually link this to the human-agent platform in Part Four — moving from a pure-AI environment to a mixed human-agent one.

## Slide 12: IV. Study 1 — Retrofitting a Legacy Platform with LLM Agents

Part Four is about building the experimental infrastructure. The first study took an existing social media platform and added LLM agents to it, so that humans and agents can coexist and interact. The platform is now retrofitted and the agents are running. Next, I will run the first pilot experiments, go through IRB approval, and set up user recruitment.

## Slide 13: IV. Study 2 — Building a New Human-Agent Social Platform

The second study is more ambitious. I am building a new social media platform from scratch, designed specifically for human and LLM-agent coexistence. This will serve as experimental infrastructure for behavioral science and social simulation. Development is still in progress. The next steps are to finish the core interaction modules, design the first pilot experiment, and build the data pipeline that connects to the norm emergence study in Part Three.
